Why More Graduates Are Choosing Apprenticeships Over University

Education & Careers Quarterly — Spring Edition

For decades, the conventional path for academically high-achieving school leavers was straightforward: university first, career second. That consensus is weakening. A growing number of students who achieve the grades to attend Russell Group universities are instead choosing higher and degree apprenticeships offered by leading employers — and the data suggests their reasoning is sound.

The financial case is increasingly compelling. A student completing a three-year degree in 2025 will graduate with average debt exceeding £45,000. A degree apprentice on the same three-year pathway earns a salary throughout, pays no tuition fees, and leaves with the same qualification. Employers including PwC, Rolls-Royce, and KPMG have reported a marked increase in the quality of applicants to their apprenticeship programmes.

Beyond the finances, employers report that degree apprentices tend to develop professional skills more rapidly than graduate hires who joined after three years away from the workplace. "The ability to immediately translate learning into practice gives them a head start that persists for several years," said one senior HR director at a FTSE 100 company.

Critics argue that the university experience has an intrinsic social and intellectual value that cannot be replicated — and that not all disciplines are served equally well by an apprenticeship pathway. Humanities, law, and medicine are cited as areas where the traditional route remains preferable.

The Office Chair Market Is Quietly Booming

Interior & Workplace Design Review — April Edition

Few product categories have benefited more from the hybrid working revolution than the humble office chair. What was once an afterthought — a standard item ordered by facilities managers in bulk and rarely replaced — has become a considered consumer purchase, driven by workers equipping home offices to a standard once reserved for corporate headquarters.

Market research firm Vantage Insight reports that UK sales of ergonomic office seating grew by 38% between 2020 and 2024, with the premium segment — chairs priced above £400 — growing fastest at 54%. The shift reflects a broader consumer willingness to invest in workspace quality, particularly among knowledge workers who spend eight or more hours a day at a desk.

Manufacturers have responded by accelerating product development cycles. Swedish firm Nordseat AB released three new models in 2024 alone, each developed in collaboration with occupational health specialists. Its Meridian Pro model, priced at £649, sold out within six weeks of launch. "The buyer is now informed, often having researched online for weeks before purchasing," said Nordseat's UK sales director, Charlotte Wyatt.

Retailers report that customers now ask detailed questions about lumbar support, seat depth adjustment, and breathability — questions that would have been unheard of a decade ago. Whether the boom will outlast the hybrid working era remains to be seen, but for now the market shows no signs of slowing.
