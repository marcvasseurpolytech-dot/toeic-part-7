Slowdown in Global Shipping Lifts Pressure on Supply Chains

International Trade Review — March Issue

After two years of unprecedented congestion and capacity shortfalls, the global container shipping industry is entering a period of relative normalisation. Freight rates, which surged to historic highs during the pandemic-related disruption of 2021 and 2022, have returned to levels closer to their long-run averages, offering relief to importers and exporters across sectors.

The easing has been driven by a combination of factors: a significant increase in vessel capacity as a wave of new container ships — ordered during the boom — entered service; a moderation in consumer demand for manufactured goods; and gradual improvements in port throughput efficiency following infrastructure investments made in response to the earlier crisis.

For businesses that rely on imported components or raw materials, the lower freight costs represent a meaningful improvement in margins, particularly for small and medium-sized enterprises that lacked the negotiating power to secure favourable rates during the crunch period. "We are now paying roughly a third of what we were paying in early 2022 for the same routes," said one operations director at a UK electronics manufacturer.

Analysts caution, however, that the current stability is not guaranteed. Ongoing geopolitical tensions in key shipping corridors, combined with the potential for renewed demand surges, mean that supply chain teams are advised to maintain diversified sourcing strategies rather than returning to single-supplier dependency.

The Rise of the Chief Sustainability Officer

Corporate Governance Quarterly — Spring Edition

A decade ago, sustainability was largely the preserve of communications departments — a function focused on producing glossy reports and managing reputational risk. Today, it is rapidly becoming a strategic boardroom priority, and with it has come the emergence of a new executive role: the Chief Sustainability Officer.

According to data published by the Governance Institute, the proportion of FTSE 250 companies with a dedicated CSO or equivalent role increased from 11% in 2018 to 43% in 2024. The growth has been driven by a convergence of regulatory pressure — particularly from the UK's mandatory TCFD climate reporting requirements — investor expectations, and the growing recognition that sustainability risks are financial risks.

The profile of the role has also evolved. Early CSOs were often recruited from environmental consultancy or public affairs backgrounds. Increasingly, however, companies are appointing candidates with operational or financial backgrounds who can integrate sustainability targets directly into business planning and capital allocation decisions. "We need someone who can sit in a room with the CFO and speak the same language," one FTSE 100 chairman told this publication.

Critics argue that in many organisations, the CSO role remains largely cosmetic — a high-profile appointment that lacks the operational authority to drive real change. Independent audits of CSO mandates suggest that in roughly a third of cases, the CSO has no direct budget control or right of veto over investment decisions.

CUSTOMER ONBOARDING PROCESS REVIEW

Prepared by: Customer Experience Team | Date: 14 March | Distribution: Senior Management

Purpose: This report reviews the current customer onboarding process following a 12-week audit. The audit was prompted by an increase in churn during the first 90 days, which rose from 8% to 14% over the past financial year.

Key findings:

1. Time to activation: The average time from contract sign to account activation is currently 11 days. Industry benchmark is 5 days. Delays are primarily caused by manual data entry requirements in the legacy system, which requires three separate logins and manual re-entry of data across platforms.

2. Welcome communications: 61% of new customers reported receiving no personalised welcome communication within the first week. Customers who did receive a welcome contact in week one showed a 34% lower churn rate in the first 90 days.

3. Support availability: Onboarding support is currently available Monday to Friday, 9:00 A.M. to 5:00 P.M. only. 42% of new customers attempted to access support outside these hours and received no response.

Recommendations: (1) Migrate the onboarding process to the new CRM platform, targeting activation in 3 days. (2) Implement an automated personalised welcome sequence triggered at contract sign. (3) Extend support availability to include weekday evenings and Saturday mornings.

Day Rates Climb as Demand for Independent Consultants Rises

The market for independent creative consultants — including copywriters, brand strategists, and UX designers — continues to tighten, with day rates rising by an average of 12% over the past 12 months according to the latest Freelance Benchmark Survey. The increase reflects both strong post-pandemic demand from businesses that reduced in-house capacity and a shrinking pool of highly experienced independents willing to take on project work.

Senior copywriters with more than eight years' experience are now commanding day rates between £550 and £750 in London, with strategists and UX specialists at similar or higher levels. Mid-level practitioners typically fall in the £350–£500 range. Rate premiums of 20–30% apply for work requiring specialist sector knowledge, such as financial services, pharmaceutical, or legal copywriting.
