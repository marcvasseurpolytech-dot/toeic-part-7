Questions 168–171 — Magazine Article

The Rise of the Four-Day Work Week

BusinessWeek Europe — October Edition

What was once dismissed as an idealistic experiment is rapidly becoming a mainstream business policy. A growing number of companies across Europe and North America have adopted the four-day work week, and the results, according to researchers at the University of Cambridge, are striking.

The Cambridge study, which tracked 61 companies over six months, found that employee productivity either held steady or increased in 92% of participating organisations. Staff reported significantly lower levels of burnout and a 34% improvement in work-life balance scores. Perhaps most tellingly, none of the companies involved in the trial chose to revert to a five-day model after it concluded.

Among the early adopters, Redfield Financial Services in Dublin stands out. After introducing the four-day week in January, the company reported a 17% reduction in staff turnover within the first year. CEO Margaret Cullen attributed this largely to improved morale and the reduction in commuting time for employees.

Critics, however, raise concerns about client-facing industries where reduced availability could damage relationships. Service providers and logistics companies in particular have argued that the model requires careful adaptation to avoid disruption.

Despite these reservations, analysts predict that the four-day week will become standard practice in knowledge-intensive sectors within the decade.

INTERNAL REPORT — CUSTOMER SERVICE DIVISION

Prepared by: Diana Chen, Operations Analyst | Date: 15 September | Distribution: Management Team

Subject: Q3 Customer Satisfaction Survey Results

This report summarises findings from the Q3 customer satisfaction survey, which was completed by 1,247 respondents across all product lines between July 1 and August 31.

Overall Satisfaction: 74% of respondents rated their overall experience as "good" or "excellent," a 6-percentage-point improvement over Q2. The strongest performance was recorded in the Returns & Refunds category (81% positive), which reflects the streamlined process introduced in June.

Areas of Concern: Response time for technical support queries received the lowest satisfaction score at 52%. Free-text comments indicate that average wait times of over 20 minutes during peak hours are the primary cause of dissatisfaction. Additionally, 38% of respondents indicated that the online help portal is "difficult to navigate."

Recommendations: (1) Hire two additional technical support agents to reduce peak-hour wait times. (2) Commission a UX audit of the online help portal. (3) Introduce a callback option for customers waiting longer than 15 minutes.

Next Steps: A follow-up survey will be conducted at the end of Q4 to measure progress. A presentation of these findings will be delivered to the full management team on 2 October.
